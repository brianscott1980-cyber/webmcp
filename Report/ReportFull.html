OpenAI went shopping and social this week with the announcement of two landmark products, 1) “Instant Checkout” in ChatGPT, the first “agentic” shopping offering available from a major AI lab, and 2) Sora, a stand-alone social app with an entirely AI-generated social feed that will compete with Meta’s Vibes. The former widens the aperture of monetization for OpenAI — a big positive given the company’s escalating AI infrastructure/compute demands ($400bn+ investment planned over next 3 years) — while Sora lays the groundwork for a potential advertising model similar to Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, and TikTok. In recent weeks, the company also announced updates to its AI infrastructure JV Stargate — now ahead of schedule — which has been bolstered by a $100bn investment from NVIDIA, and a new MOU signed with Microsoft on Sep 11 (incrementally derisking OpenAI’s PBC-transition). We also share our latest thoughts on enterprise, risks, and the upcoming DevDay.

Monetization: Buy It in ChatGPT

ChatGPT’s new “Instant Checkout” feature will enable ChatGPT users to purchase products directly within the chat interface. This feature operates via a newly created open standard, Agentic Commerce Protocol (ACP) — developed in partnership with leading digital payment processor Stripe — which integrates orders from ChatGPT into backend merchant payment and fulfillment systems and Shared Payment Tokens (SPTs) issued by Stripe to enable the secure transfer of credentials and transactions. OpenAI notes that the results will be “organic and unsponsored, ranked purely on relevance to the user,” with merchants charged an affiliate fee on completed purchases (our channel checks suggest ~4% avg, with lower fees for larger scale merchants/platforms). Data suggests 2.1% of consumer queries on ChatGPT relate to seeking information on purchasable products, and with ~29,000 messages sent per second on ChatGPT as of July, we expect the e-commerce opportunity has substantial upside from the current ~50mn daily product-related queries. Survey data from Alpharoc supports this claim, with 10.6% of users in August reporting they used “ChatGPT/LLMs” as a primary source for product information, up from 4.4% in January.

Over time, we expect that accumulated purchasing data on ChatGPT will allow for a more personalized e-commerce experience where the model learns an individual’s preferences (e.g., cost vs quality, interests), increasing the value of targeted digital ads — if the company chooses to go this route — and potentially paving a path to “fully” agentic purchases, where an agent autonomously purchases products without a user’s input, based on a deep understanding of a user’s preferences and needs.

Monetization: Soraing into the Future

OpenAI’s new standalone Sora app — powered by the company’s newly released Sora 2 model (Sora was the name of its gen 1 video model) — enables users to scroll through an entirely AI-generated short-form video feed, while embedding cameos of themselves and their friends into their creations. Addressing monetization, the Sora 2 release notes proclaimed, “a lot of problems with other apps stem from the monetization model incentivizing decisions that are at odds with user wellbeing. Transparently, our only current plan is to eventually give users the option to pay some amount to generate an extra video if there’s too much demand relative to available compute. As the app evolves, we will openly communicate any changes in our approach here.”The offering, which appears similar to TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts based on the company’s Sep 30 demo, potentially opens the door to high CPM advertising with solid placement for video-generated ads (including AI-generated content that could reduce ad production costs) while enabling a more seamless integration for ads than an AI chatbot (where trust erosion is a greater concern).Given ChatGPT’s >700mn WAUs (800mn WAUs reported by investors in a recent podcast), we see the potential for rapid adoption, and beyond advertising, another mechanism for the company to acquire unique consumer data.

Infrastructure: “Everything Starts with Compute” – Sam Altman

OpenAI recently announced a strategic partnership with NVIDIA to deploy 10GW of NVIDIA systems, alongside a $100bn investment in OpenAI (invested progressively as each GW is deployed). NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang noted the investment does not include any obligation for OpenAI to purchase NVIDIA chips (note NVIDIA is covered by J.P. Morgan analyst Harlan Sur). Investors have been asking what this relationship means for OpenAI’s custom AI silicon efforts with Broadcom, given earlier this month Broadcom announced that a "qualified customer" (believed to be OpenAI) secured $10bn in orders (note Broadcom is covered by J.P. Morgan analyst Harlan Sur). Our view remains that it is not a question of GPUs or custom AI silicon, rather that OpenAI will continue to use the most performant chips in training (currently NVIDIA GPUs), and for inference deployment, will look to optimize cost efficiency, which is likely to involve the use of custom AI silicon.

Following the NVIDIA news, OpenAI announced five new data center sites plus an expanded CoreWeave partnership, which collectively brings Stargate to nearly 7GW of planned capacity, putting the 10GW commitment they announced in Jan-25, ahead of schedule. This should enable OpenAI’s ability to remain at the frontier of training while servicing its growing user base in the coming years, however, we still see power as a potential bottleneck for data center development.

Financials: Escalating Costs Demand Bold Top-Line Targets

OpenAI’s revenue is reportedly expected to reach $200bn by 2030, according to The Information, up from $13bn this year (previous reports touted $174bn by 2030). The Information notes OpenAI expects to derive $90bn in revenue from ChatGPT by 2030, and that cumulative revenue between 2026-2030 from new products could be ~$110bn, reinforcing the growing expectation that entirely new products (we assume including hardware) will serve as significant drivers of growth in the next few years. We see digital ads and in-app shopping as natural extensions for products like Sora and ChatGPT Pulse.

“Vibe spending” continues with OpenAI reportedly expected to burn $115bn in cash between 2025-2029 (per The Information), driven by rising computing and server costs. OpenAI announced a partnership with Oracle that exceeds $300bn over the next five years, and an expanded CoreWeave agreement from $15.9bn to $22.4bn in aggregate (note Oracle and CoreWeave are covered by J.P. Morgan analyst Mark Murphy). Given the scale of these contracts, despite significant capital investments from partners including NVIDIA and SoftBank, OpenAI's ambitious ramp-up in revenue growth will be essential to meeting the company's obligations (projected figures translate into ~73% CAGR 2025-30, from starting point of $13bn).

Model Competition: Going Head-to-Head with Anthropic

Competition across key enterprise platforms continues to escalate with Anthropic’s models now available on Microsoft’s 365 Copilot, directly competing with OpenAI models. Media outlets recently reported a $100mn multi-year partnership between OpenAI and Databricks to make OpenAI models available to 20,000+ Databricks customers (a move set to erode share from Anthropic, which announced a five-year strategic partnership with Databricks in March). As distribution channels converge, we expect the intersection of price and performance to be an increasingly pivotal buying factor for enterprises.

Enterprise-relevant model capabilities also continue to expand. On Sep 25, OpenAI released a research paper on GDPval, a new benchmark to evaluate AI model performance on “real-world economically valuable tasks.” Anthropic’s Claude Opus 4.1 led with a 47.6% combined win/tie rate relative to experts, achieving near parity with human experts from a capability perspective. OpenAI’s GPT-5 ranked second with a 38.8% win/tie rate, while Google’s Gemini 2.5 Pro and xAI’s Grok 4 lagged with 25.5% and 24.3% win/tie rates, respectively. Broadly, the study reinforces our view of Claude as the leading enterprise solution while also highlighting some of the challenges that still stand between further AI-enabled productivity, automation, and workforce replacement.

Risks: Path to PBC Transition Easing

As highlighted in our initiation note, OpenAI’s pending PBC transition brings uncertainty to the company’s fundraising plans ($20bn of $40bn March fundraise contingent on a “recapitalization of the economic waterfall”), and Microsoft’s agreement to the transition remains a key dependency (note Microsoft is covered by J.P. Morgan analyst Mark Murphy). On Sep 11, OpenAI announced that a non-binding memorandum of understanding (MOU) had been signed between the two companies, outlining the next phase of their partnership. While the PBC transition is still pending regulatory approval, the MOU between OpenAI and Microsoft derisks a significant hurdle to OpenAI’s successful transition to a PBC.

Risks: Increased Regulatory Scrutiny on Child Safety

On Sep 11, the FTC launched an inquiry into seven consumer-facing AI-powered chatbots, including those from OpenAI, Alphabet, Meta, Snap, and xAI. This came weeks after a widely publicized tragedy involving a teenager following conversations with ChatGPT. This has prompted OpenAI to improve transparency for parents, release greater parental controls (setting blackout hours, disabling features, and guiding how ChatGPT responds to their teen), and build an age-prediction system to estimate users’ ages (conservatively) so that they can provide an “under-18 experience” to non-adult users. We expect to see continued scrutiny from regulators and stakeholders as LLM adoption continues to proliferate.

Upcoming: OpenAI DevDay – Oct 6

With a slew of recent product releases (Instant Checkout, Sora, ChatGPT Pulse) leading up to OpenAI’s developer conference in San Francisco next week (Oct 6), we find it helpful to review releases from past DevDay events. Last year, OpenAI unveiled its most powerful model (at the time), o1 — a successor to o1-preview — in addition to announcing reduced API pricing for specific models, and new developer features such as preference fine-tuning. The year prior (the company’s first developer day), they also released a new model (GPT-4 Turbo) as well as new multimodal capabilities with an updated image creation model and text-to-speech (TTS) — which later became a key component of ChatGPT Voice Mode. The recent release of a series of “OpenAI on OpenAI” videos showcasing a variety of internal products that help boost productivity related to customer support, data analytics, and sales, could hint to the upcoming release of more specialized, enterprise-focused tools. Given proximity to the release of GPT-5 (and Sora 2), we expect any model release will likely be incremental — such as an enhanced version of GPT-5 available in the API — however, the track record of past DevDay releases opens the door to more material model and product announcements.
